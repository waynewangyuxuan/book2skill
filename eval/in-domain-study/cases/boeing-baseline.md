# Boeing (BA): Equity Research Note — Call: HOLD (with a Buy bias on weakness below ~$200)

**Price:** ~$226 | **52-wk range:** $177–$254 | **Consensus PT:** $270 | **Date:** June 17, 2026

## The thesis in one line
Boeing is a credible turnaround mid-flight: the operational story is real, the backlog is enormous, but the stock already discounts a successful recovery while the balance sheet and 777X still carry meaningful execution risk. That asymmetry — limited upside to the $270 consensus target (~20%) against a stock that has already round-tripped from the crisis lows — argues for holding existing positions and accumulating on dips rather than chasing here.

## What's working (the bull case)
The recovery is no longer a promise; it's showing in the numbers. Q1 2026 revenue rose 14% YoY to $22.2B, operating margin turned positive at ~2%, and core loss per share narrowed to just $0.20 — a steep improvement from the multi-dollar losses of the dark years. Deliveries are climbing (60 aircraft in May), and management is guiding 737 production to 47/month by this summer and 52/month later in the year. That cadence is the single most important variable for Boeing: cash is generated on delivery, and each incremental MAX off the line drops high-margin revenue straight toward free cash flow.

The demand backdrop is exceptional. The order backlog is a record ~$695B (BCA ~$576B, 6,000+ commercial jets; BDS a record $86B). This is not a demand problem — it's a supply/execution problem, which is the better problem to have. Defense (BDS) grew 21% to $7.6B and is guided to cash-flow breakeven in 2026–2027, removing a chronic drag. The recent first major China order in nearly a decade reopens a structurally important market. Full-year FCF guidance of $1–3B, with the heavy inflows back-half weighted, would mark Boeing's first positive cash year since the crisis — a genuine inflection.

## What's not (the bear case)
The balance sheet remains the overhang. Gross debt is $47.2B (some sources cite ~$54B all-in), against $20.9B of cash and a $10B undrawn revolver. Management deserves credit for paying down $6.9B in the quarter, but this is still a heavily levered industrial that, critically, *diluted shareholders by issuing 112.5 million shares last year* to shore up the balance sheet. That dilution permanently shrinks each holder's claim on the eventual $10B FCF prize, and is the clearest tell that the recovery was financed from a position of weakness.

The 777X is the live landmine. Management itself warned the coming year will be "difficult," with the program consuming cash and no improvement expected before 2027–2028; the Rolls-Royce engine issues and Emirates' visible impatience underscore continued certification/timeline risk. The 737-7 and 737-10 certifications are also still pending (year-end target, deliveries in 2027) — slippage there has been the rule, not the exception, at Boeing. And Q1 FCF was *negative $1.5B*; the entire positive-FCF year rests on a back-half hockey-stick that depends on the production ramp landing on schedule through a still-fragile supply chain.

## Valuation and the call
Sentiment is already constructive — roughly 21 buy-equivalent ratings against 5 holds and 1 sell, consensus PT $270. The aggressive bull cases ($365, or the fanciful $3,240-by-2030 model) require flawless execution and the full $10B FCF run-rate to be capitalized at a premium multiple; they are upside optionality, not a base case to underwrite at today's price. On near-term fundamentals, the stock screens expensive — it doesn't earn on a P/E basis, and EV/sales already embeds the recovery. The honest base case: ~20% to consensus over twelve months, gated on milestones (47/month rate, MAX-7/10 cert, a positive-FCF print) that are *expected* and therefore largely priced.

**Verdict: HOLD.** The risk/reward is roughly symmetric here. The turnaround is real enough that shorting or selling outright is the wrong move — you'd be fighting a record backlog, improving cash, and a methodical management rebuild. But the leverage, dilution, 777X cash burn, and a valuation that already credits the recovery cap the upside and leave little margin for the inevitable Boeing-style slippage. For existing holders: stay long, this is a recovery worth riding. For new capital: be patient. I'd turn to an outright **Buy below ~$200** (closer to the 52-week low), where you're paid to underwrite the execution risk; above $240–$250 the setup tilts toward trimming. Concrete triggers to upgrade: a clean positive-FCF Q2 print on July 29, confirmation of the 47/month rate, and MAX-7/10 certification on schedule.

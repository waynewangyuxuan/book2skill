## When to use
Any competitive forecasting or analysis task where a crowd of capable peers already produces a baseline estimate and your value comes only from being differentially right.

## Method
1. Establish the consensus/reference estimate first and treat it as a strong, usually-correct prior — the crowd is more often right than its critics claim.
2. Locate the specific points where your independent view diverges from that baseline.
3. Pour effort into those divergence points; deliberately under-invest where you would merely reproduce consensus.
4. Weight effort by (size of your divergence) × (the variable's impact), concentrating where opportunity dispersion is largest.
5. Expect honest analysis to agree with consensus most of the time — treat genuine divergence as rare and precious, and never manufacture contrarianism for its own sake.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: a reference/consensus estimate + your independent view + per-variable impact.
- Outputs: a ranked list of high-value divergence points to research.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Reflexive contrarianism: manufacturing dissent to feel differentiated when the crowd is right.
- Mistaking comprehensiveness for edge — marginal value lives only at the frontier.
- Requires a genuinely informed consensus to diverge from; a thin baseline is not a real reference class.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
Expert practitioners, when surveyed, concentrate on the factors where their view differs from the crowd; novices spread effort evenly across comprehensive coverage and generate no edge.

## When to use
Estimating how many competitors a market will sustain, or judging whether market growth will strengthen or weaken an incumbent's scale advantage.

## Method
1. Estimate the ratio of fixed cost to market size for the relevant (often local or niche) arena — not the industry's name.
2. Predict sustainable competitor count from that ratio: a small bounded market that funds only one efficient operator yields a natural monopoly the rational second entrant avoids; the identical economics in a large market dissolves into fragmentation; intermediate sizes fall in between.
3. For a scale-advantaged incumbent, treat market growth as a threat, not a friend: as the market expands, fixed costs stay constant while variable costs scale, so fixed cost falls as a share of total for everyone, compressing the incumbent's percentage-cost edge and lowering the share an entrant needs to reach viability.
4. Invert the usual heuristics: "small/bounded market" is structurally advantaged for monopoly; "big growing market" predictably dissolves a scale moat.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: fixed-cost level, relevant market size, growth trajectory.
- Out: predicted concentration and the direction in which growth moves the incumbent's advantage.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Requires correctly bounding the "relevant market" (where fixed costs stay fixed); mis-bounding wrecks the ratio.
- Applies to scale-based advantages; cost-of-capital or pure-captivity advantages are not eroded by share loss the same way.
- Network effects and switching costs can blunt fragmentation even in large markets.

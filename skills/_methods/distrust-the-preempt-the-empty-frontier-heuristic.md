## When to use
Considering a first-mover land-grab into genuinely unclaimed (no-incumbent) geographic or product territory, relying on "first to commit preempts the field."

## Method
1. Recognize the information-signaling mechanism: a pioneer's irreversible commitment to an empty market signals to equally-strong rivals that the opportunity is real — that is information, not deterrence, and it invites rather than repels entry.
2. Note the asymmetric career/competitive risk: a slightly-later follower faces less downside than a pioneer who failed alone, so rivals are happy to follow a validated success.
3. Treat unoccupied "frontiers" as likely to attract co-entrants; weight discretion over the land-grab unless your time-lead confers a genuinely durable advantage (not merely a small head start).

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: an unclaimed territory + your would-be lead + the strength of likely rivals.
- Out: a corrected (usually more cautious) expectation of whether first-commitment actually preempts.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- If the early move builds a real, durable barrier (lock-up of a scarce input, strong network effect), preemption can genuinely work — distinguish that from a bare head start.
- Applies to roughly equally-strong rivals; against much weaker followers a lead may suffice.

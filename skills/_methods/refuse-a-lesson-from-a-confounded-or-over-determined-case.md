## When to use
When a case study teaches a lesson but the outcome had multiple co-occurring sufficient causes — the actor's own mistake *and* an exogenous shock — or when alternatives were "ruled out" one by one and the framework's favored cause conveniently survived by elimination.

## Method
1. List *all* plausible sufficient causes of the outcome, not just the one the author highlights.
2. **Over-determination check:** would the confounder alone have produced the outcome? If yes, the case can't isolate the favored cause, so it doesn't actually support the lesson.
3. **Eliminative check:** when explanations are dispatched in sequence and the residual is assigned to the favorite, ask whether the survivor was *independently measured* or merely credited with whatever the others couldn't explain.
4. Downgrade the case from "evidence for the lesson" to "consistent with the lesson but confounded."

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: a case + the lesson it's used to support.
- Out: the full cause list, a verdict on whether the favored cause is isolated or confounded, and whether the surviving explanation was independently measured.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Eliminative reasoning is legitimate when the candidate list is genuinely complete and the survivor is corroborated — check completeness and corroboration, don't reject elimination wholesale.

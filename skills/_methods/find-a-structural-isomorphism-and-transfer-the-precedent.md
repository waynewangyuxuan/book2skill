## When to use
Facing an unfamiliar business/system and wanting to reuse hard-won lessons from a known one.

## Method
1. Strip both the unfamiliar case and candidate analogues to their dominant cost drivers: where do fixed costs concentrate? Are they local? Is there customer captivity?
2. Match skeletons: if the structural drivers align, the cases are isomorphic regardless of surface dissimilarity.
3. Transfer the known case's strategic prescriptions and characteristic failure modes directly to the new case.
4. Explain any divergence in actual outcomes between isomorphic cases by differences in their environment (e.g. strength of rivals), not by differences in their structure.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: an unfamiliar system + a library of understood cases.
- Out: a matched analogue, transferred prescriptions/failure modes, and an environment-based account of any outcome gap.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Superficial analogy: matching on surface features instead of the cost-economic skeleton imports the wrong lessons.
- Two cases can share structure yet diverge so much in environment that the transfer is weak — the divergence step must actually be done, not skipped.
- Requires a stocked library of well-understood reference cases.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A regional brewer and a regional discount retailer look unrelated, but both reduce to the same skeleton: regional fixed-cost economies of scale in distribution and advertising plus local customer inertia. The retailer's lessons (defend the dense home region; don't disperse) transfer to the brewer; the brewer's worse outcome is then explained by a stronger rival in its arena, not by a different structure.

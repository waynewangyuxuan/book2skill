## When to use
When you face a current multi-player situation and need to predict whether it is stable or about to shift — and how reliable that "stability" is over a long horizon.

## Method
1. Lay out the players, their available actions, and the payoff each combination yields.
2. **Stability of expectations:** does each player believe the others will keep their current choice?
3. **Stability of behavior:** given that belief, can any single player improve its payoff by unilaterally changing? The state is a stable equilibrium only if NO player can improve alone; if any can, that move is coming.
4. **Act on it:** if the predicted move hurts you, prepare for it or change the payoffs first (e.g. pre-commit to a response that removes the mover's incentive).
5. **Turnover stress test:** the model abstracts away human variables (leadership personality, incentive systems, culture). Ask whether a plausible change in *one* — a new decision-maker, a new bonus scheme — would flip the outcome, and check the historical record for whether the "equilibrium" actually held or repeatedly collapsed and reformed with personnel changes. If a single agent change flips it, relabel "equilibrium" as "leadership-contingent truce" and discount its reliability for any long-horizon decision.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: a current situation with identified players, actions, and payoffs + the agents sustaining it.
- Outputs: a stable/unstable verdict (if unstable, the predicted move and a pre-emptive option) and a robustness rating against agent/leadership turnover.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Assuming a comfortable-looking state is stable without checking each player's unilateral incentive.
- Payoffs must reflect real motivations (possibly relative performance, not just profit) — adjust them.
- Some equilibria are institutionally locked (contracts, structure) and survive turnover; check for those locks before declaring fragility, and estimate the probability of a flip rather than assuming the worst.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A cooperative high-price truce among a few rivals passed the no-unilateral-improvement test on paper, but the historical record showed it had collapsed each time a share-driven CEO took over one player — so it was a leadership-contingent truce, not an equilibrium to plan a long horizon around.

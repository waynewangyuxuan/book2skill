# Model a conserved system as equal-and-opposite

## When to use
When the sum of a quantity over all parts of a system is fixed (zero or constant) and you are tempted to analyze one part in isolation, or one member is blamed one-sidedly for a shared-unit deviation.

## Method
1. Confirm the conservation law (parts sum to a constant).
2. Express any local change as forcing an equal-and-opposite change in the complement.
3. For a "glut"/"surplus" claim, look for a redistribution across parts, not a rise in the global total — the total cannot rise net.
4. In a shared-unit system, note that one member's deviation from fair value is necessarily mirrored by an opposite deviation among the others; reject one-sided blame.
5. Forecast a "local" outcome from the dominant counterparty's move, since the system forces the opposite on everyone else regardless of their preference.

## Inputs -> Outputs
Inputs: the conserved aggregate; a local change or a dominant-party move. Outputs: the forced complementary change; a redistribution map.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Treating a redistribution as a level change (and wrongly "refuting" a glut because the total didn't rise).
- The identity fixes the magnitudes but not the direction of causality.

## Worked example
A surplus in one bloc is shown to force an exactly offsetting deficit elsewhere; a policy to cut one side's balance must therefore move some counterparty's balance the other way.

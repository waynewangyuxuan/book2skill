# Discount misleading evidence — outliers, bundles, and contradictions

## When to use
When weighing evidence and it is anecdotal, drawn from a "failed" precedent, splits contradictorily, or rests on a refuted aggregate.

## Method
1. **Outlier-as-anchor.** If one vivid example recurs across many arguments, suspect it is selected for being atypical; check it against its class's central tendency before letting it anchor a generalization.
2. **Policy-bundle confound.** When a lever "failed," look for a simultaneous counter-policy that cushioned its side-effects; attribute the null to the bundle, not the lever's impotence.
3. **Contradiction-as-confound.** When single-factor studies give opposite signs, suspect an omitted compensating channel; resolve by reasoning through the closed system, not by adding more single-variable studies.
4. **Conserved-quantity refutation check.** When a hypothesis is dismissed because an aggregate didn't move, check whether it actually predicts a redistribution; the right observable is the cross-sectional shift, not the total.

## Inputs -> Outputs
Inputs: the evidence and the claim it bears on. Outputs: a corrected weight on each piece, with the flaw named.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Over-applying can let you dismiss any inconvenient evidence; require a specific named defect.
- A genuine null sometimes really is a null.

## Worked example
A "this lever doesn't work" claim is rebutted by finding the offsetting policy bundled with it in the cited failed case.

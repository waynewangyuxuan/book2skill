## When to use
When choosing where to compete or how to grow, especially when tempted by large distant markets, or when forecasting whether an expansion will hold its gains.

## Method
1. Bound the arena narrowly — by geography OR by product/feature space; treat the two identically, because the underlying economics (covering fixed costs within a bounded arena) is the same.
2. Achieve clear dominance inside that bound before expanding; verify the dominance is real first.
3. Consolidate the gains and defend the base aggressively against any incursion.
4. Expand only to immediately adjacent territory where existing strength still reaches; consolidate each new ring before advancing.
5. Refuse discontinuous leaps into distant unbounded markets: dispersion raises your costs and invites rivals into your undefended base — you lose on two fronts.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: candidate markets, the home arena, a growth ambition, incumbent strength in each target.
- Outputs: a sequenced expansion path radiating from a dominated core, with the base defended — and a judgment of where a proposed expansion is likely to break.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Assumes the advantage is local/fixed-cost-driven; genuinely global advantages (rare) relax the constraint.
- "Adjacent" is hard to define in abstract product space.
- A disciplined edge-expander can still be beaten by a stronger rival in new territory; the pattern governs durability of the approach, not guaranteed victory.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A discounter that wins one region, fills in adjacent counties ring by ring, and only then moves outward stays dominant; a same-structure player that jumps coast-to-coast into entrenched rivals gets beaten there while its home base is exposed.

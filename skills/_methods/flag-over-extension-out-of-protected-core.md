## When to use
Evaluating an expansion or diversification plan, especially one driven by a "grow or die" imperative or internal ambition rather than a structural opportunity.

## Method
1. Locate the actor's protected core (where it has a real barrier/advantage).
2. Check whether the proposed move expands that protected core or leaves it to enter arenas where the actor is a weak newcomer fighting entrenched incumbents.
3. If the latter, assign membership in the high-base-rate failure reference class — two mirror forms: a dominant firm over-diversifying out of its protected core into unrelated arenas, and a disciplined small player abandoning a working niche to scale into incumbents' territory. Both predictably destroy value because the cost/cooperation advantages do not travel.
4. Locate the driver. For small disciplined players the proximate cause of death is usually INTERNAL (founder identity, growth-as-mandate, employee expectations), not external opportunity — the recurring arc is: discipline succeeds → internal pressure to grow → expansion into strong incumbents' core → loss of the cost/cooperation advantages → collapse.
5. Treat the discipline-abandonment moment, not the market, as the likely cause of failure; contrast with the disciplined edge-expander pattern (win core, expand only contiguously) to get a directional rule.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: an expansion plan + the actor's protected core + the plan's driver.
- Out: a base-rate-informed risk flag and identification of the (often internal) failure driver.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Some over-extensions succeed when the new arena shares the core's actual advantage — distinguish genuine adjacency from superficial relatedness.
- A correctly-executed edge-expansion can still fail against a stronger rival; the reference class informs the prior, not the verdict.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A disciplined regional player thrived on local scale and incumbent tolerance; under internal growth pressure it leapt nationally into entrenched rivals, lost the local-scale and cooperation advantages that protected it, and bled margins for years — the abandonment of the working core, not any external shock, was the cause.

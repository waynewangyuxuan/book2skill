## When to use
Comparing the performance of similar actors, or explaining why an actor's performance changed, without over-attributing to structure or to luck.

## Method
1. Decompose any performance comparison into two independent engines before assigning causes:
   - **Structural position / frontier:** does a barrier/advantage exist, and where is the achievable productivity frontier? These inputs (capital stock, labor quality, diffusing technology) move slowly and essentially only upward.
   - **Execution / gap-closing:** how far inside the frontier is the actor operating? This is driven by focused management attention and moves fast, in both directions.
2. Note that most actors lack any structural advantage yet show durable 2–3× gaps among structurally-identical peers — that variance is execution, not technology/capital/labor, and the high-leverage move is usually closing the gap, not pushing the frontier. Look for unexploited slack first.
3. **Attribute a change by matching timescales:** a sharp swing within months (especially bidirectional) cannot come from the slow structural inputs — it implicates management attention/focus. A slow steady drift implicates structural inputs.
4. Use the mismatch between the brisk pace of observed change and the glacial pace of structural change as a fingerprint test for "it was execution, not the environment" — after checking for any genuine one-off structural shock (regulation, discontinuous technology).

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: a performance comparison or change + its timescale + the candidate causes.
- Outputs: an attribution split between structural position and execution, with a tempo-based fingerprint for the cause of any change and the implied lever to pull.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- The two engines can interact (good execution can build toward a barrier); treat them as separable for attribution, not fully independent in reality.
- Genuine one-off structural shocks can occasionally produce fast change — check for them before concluding "execution."

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
Former sibling utilities using identical equipment, labor contracts, and technology showed up to 40% persistent productivity gaps — explainable only by execution. And one of them tripled productivity in two weeks during a labor disruption with no new capital — a swing far too fast to be structural, fingerprinting it as attention/execution.

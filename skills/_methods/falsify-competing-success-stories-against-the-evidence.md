## When to use
Multiple plausible explanations compete to account for an actor's success or failure and you must identify the real cause.

## Method
1. Enumerate the popular explanations (including the vivid, intuitive, and self-flattering ones).
2. For each, derive what it predicts should be visible in the data.
3. Discard every explanation the evidence contradicts (e.g. "they bullied suppliers" fails if their input costs were higher; "monopoly pricing" fails if their prices were lower).
4. Keep the surviving explanation — and prefer the one that also has an a-priori structural mechanism, not merely a story.
5. Stay alert to the recurring trap: the most available, vivid, or flattering explanation is often the easiest to falsify.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: an outcome + a set of candidate explanations + the relevant data.
- Out: a falsified-down-to-one causal account with a structural mechanism.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- The true cause may not be among the enumerated candidates; eliminative induction only works over a complete-enough set.
- Multiple causes can jointly hold; forcing a single survivor oversimplifies.
- Data may be insufficient to falsify a given hypothesis cleanly.

## When to use
When a case is held up as a generalizable best practice (a cooperation scheme, a strategy that "worked"), or when advice claims the same outcome could have been reached by a legal/tacit route instead of the actual one.

## Method
1. Identify the *necessary enabling condition* for the success — the thing without which it would not have worked.
2. Ask how often that enabler recurs. If it's rare (e.g. a state-enforced permanent barrier to entry, a closed industry), the lesson is a case study, not a playbook.
3. **Counterfactual-substitute check:** when advice says "they should have achieved this legally/tacitly instead," ask whether the legal and illegal versions produce the *same* effect. If the only difference is detectability, the advice may be teaching how not to get caught — and assumes, without proof, that the tacit version is stable and durable.
4. State transferability explicitly: would the practice work *without* the rare enabler? If not, don't generalize it.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: a success case or "should-have-done-it-differently" prescription.
- Out: the necessary enabler, its rarity, and a transferability verdict (general practice vs special case).

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Identifying the "necessary" enabler is a judgment — test it by asking whether removing it plausibly breaks the outcome.
- A rare enabler doesn't make the case worthless; it bounds where the lesson applies.

## When to use
When a dramatic burst of performance (a crisis response, a fill-in during a disruption, a quarter of heroic output) is cited as proof of latent, removable inefficiency or durable productivity headroom.

## Method
1. Distinguish *peak* performance from *sustainable* performance. A sprint defers maintenance, burns goodwill, and skips lower-priority work.
2. Check what the surge quietly dropped — tasks not performed during the crisis are part of the real steady-state load.
3. Ask whether the surge level was held long enough to incur the deferred costs. If not, generalizing it into a permanent target overstates achievable improvement.
4. Separately, when a concluding caveat (e.g. "management also matters a lot") materially weakens the work's main thesis, hold the caveat to the *same* evidentiary standard as the thesis — don't let a catch-all factor support itself with anecdotes the main claim wasn't allowed to use.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: a peak-performance datum used to argue durable capacity/efficiency gains.
- Out: the sustainable-vs-peak gap, the deferred costs the surge hid, and whether the inferred headroom is real or a sprint artifact.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Some surges *do* reveal genuine removable slack; the test is duration and the accounting of deferred work, not reflexive dismissal.

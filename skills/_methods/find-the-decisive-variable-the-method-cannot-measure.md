## When to use
When a method's conclusion turns on an input that is, by the method's own admission, not directly observable — a "pure-play proxy," an assumed rival payoff/objective, an interpersonally-comparable utility, or a disagreement/threat point.

## Method
1. Identify the decisive input and confirm it's estimated/proxied rather than measured.
2. Ask the contamination question: could two honest analysts, given the same proxy freedom, reach opposite conclusions? If yes, the data does not constrain the answer.
3. For imported formal results (e.g. bargaining/fairness axioms), check that the theorem's *preconditions* actually hold in the messy domain — comparable payoffs, a known disagreement point. If the inputs can't be measured, the clean theorem gives only an illusion of objectivity.
4. For game-theoretic predictions, re-solve under 2–3 plausible alternative specifications of the unobserved input (e.g. rival maximizes relative share, not profit) and see if the prediction is stable.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: a method + its decisive proxied input.
- Out: the sensitivity of the conclusion to the proxy, and a flag if the answer is analyst-determined rather than data-determined.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Proxies are unavoidable in real analysis; the goal is to surface how much the conclusion *rides* on the proxy choice, not to forbid proxies.
- Re-specifying rival objectives requires its own judgment — document the alternatives rather than asserting one.

# Re-derive a value-laden label from its forced consequences

## When to use
When something is labeled a privilege, advantage, or generous favor, and you suspect the label is the opposite of its real incidence.

## Method
1. Trace the concrete actions the labeled condition forces on its holder.
2. Tally the costs and benefits those forced actions impose on the holder.
3. If only harms follow, the "privilege" is a misnamed burden (and vice versa).
4. Cross-check with revealed behavior: if the condition were truly a benefit, parties able to acquire it would; consistent avoidance inverts the label.
5. For an apparent "favor," check whether it is the forced by-product of the giver's own goal while the recipient bears a cost — then the giver gains and the receiver loses.

## Inputs -> Outputs
Inputs: a labeled condition; its forced consequences; revealed behavior. Outputs: the corrected sign of the label.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Mixed cases exist; weigh net incidence rather than flipping on a single harm.
- Avoidance can have other causes than the label being wrong.

## Worked example
A reserve-currency "privilege" is re-derived as a burden once you trace the forced absorption it imposes and note that others actively avoid the role.

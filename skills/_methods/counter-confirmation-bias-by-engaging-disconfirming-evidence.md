## When to use
Maintaining a thesis or reviewing an outcome, where the mind tends to seek confirmation and dodge blame.

## Method
1. Make searching for and engaging the counter-case a required, explicit step.
2. Adopt the rule that counter-evidence you cannot refute should be treated as true.
3. On a loss, default to "my process was flawed" unless you can prove otherwise; do not credit wins to skill and blame losses on luck.
4. Reverse the comfortable defaults that recast bad news as temporary — they sever the learning signal.

## Inputs to Outputs
- Inputs: a thesis or an outcome; the available disconfirming evidence.
- Outputs: an engaged counter-case and a learning-preserving attribution.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Skimming past disconfirming evidence to protect the thesis.
- Self-serving attribution that blocks learning from losses.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
After a loss, a practitioner writes down where the process failed instead of attributing it to bad luck, and catches a repeatable error.

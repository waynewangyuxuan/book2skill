## When to use
When a framework asserts an automatic corrective ("high returns inevitably attract entry that competes them away," "the market will fix this") or derives a confident factor ranking ("factor B matters, factor A doesn't") from theory rather than comparative data.

## Method
1. For any "X will inevitably happen" mechanism, list its *enabling conditions*: the actors must observe the opportunity, be able to fund the response, be able to act at scale, and do so within a relevant horizon. The mechanism fails wherever any condition is absent.
2. Note that "the long run" can exceed any decision-relevant timeframe; an inevitability with no horizon is not actionable, and frameworks often quietly admit an asymmetric/slow adjustment elsewhere.
3. For a theory-derived factor ranking, treat it as a *hypothesis*, not a finding. A dismissal of a factor based on the theory's own premise is only as good as that premise. Ask for a controlled comparison (good-A/bad-B vs bad-A/good-B) rather than a deduction plus one war story.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: an inevitability claim or a theory-driven factor ranking.
- Out: the enabling conditions and where they fail, the (often unstated) horizon, and whether a factor ranking rests on data or assumed premises.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Many corrective mechanisms *do* operate given enough time; the skill identifies *where and how slowly*, not that they never work.
- Demanding controlled data can be an impossible standard in business; accept the best available comparison while flagging deduction-only rankings as tentative.

## When to use
When a few comparable players are locked in (or sliding toward) a value-destroying war over price, features, advertising, or resources.

## Method
1. **Prefer structural changes** that alter the game itself, from a fixed menu:
   - occupy separate non-overlapping niches (geography / specialty / time-slot) so you don't compete head-on;
   - design loyalty programs whose rewards tie to cumulative purchases and accelerate with volume;
   - limit total capacity so price-cutting can't be supplied;
   - adopt most-favored-nation terms that make any cut apply to all customers;
   - compress the buying window;
   - align internal incentives on profit, not share/size.
2. **If structural levers aren't available, use the paired tactic:** (a) respond to any aggressive move immediately and ideally automatically (pre-committed match) so the defector never profits; (b) simultaneously signal willingness to return to the cooperative level. Both halves are required — punishment alone leaves everyone stuck low; the up-signal alone invites exploitation.
3. **Choose the battlefield by relative volume:** retaliate where the rival is large and you are small, so a price cut hurts them more per unit. Avoid fighting where you dominate, where each cut costs you most.
4. Keep the goal in view: restore cooperation, not win the skirmish.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Input: a few-player rivalry and the available structural/tactical levers.
- Output: a sequenced de-escalation plan (structure first, tactics second) with battlefield selection by volume asymmetry.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Failure: jumping to tactical price wars without restructuring; punishing in your own stronghold (cutting off your nose).
- Assumption: the players are protected by barriers (capacity limits etc. collapse if newcomers can enter); direct collusion is illegal — these moves are structural/tactical, not agreements to fix prices.

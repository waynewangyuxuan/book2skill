## When to use
You are an incumbent (or gatekeeper) in a protected market deciding how to respond to a new entrant — or how to posture before any appears.

## Method
1. Resist framing the decision as "how much will this one entrant cost me?"
2. Reframe it as: is this entrant a one-off exception, or the first of a wave a soft response would invite?
   - If it credibly demonstrates it is unrepeatable (unique assets/position others can't replicate), accommodation maximizes joint profit — tolerate it.
   - If letting it in signals "the door is open," fight it hard early to deter the rest, even at a loss on this one case.
3. Exploit the asymmetric burden of proof: as the defended incumbent you only need parity to hold your installed/captive base, while the entrant must achieve clear superiority to pull customers. An attacker that reaches only "as good as you" loses.
4. Pre-position credible deterrence before any entrant arrives: visible excess capacity, a high-fixed/low-variable cost structure, saturated niches, a war chest, a reputation for single-market ferocity. Note a diversified, coolly rational defender is a *weaker* deterrent than a single-minded one — and weigh the standing cost of the posture against the entry it actually prevents (deterred entrants are invisible).
5. Once an entrant has moved in, if you choose to fight, punish it at the lowest cost to yourself — in its home market, not yours.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: the entrant's replicability/uniqueness; the precedent risk; your cost structure and capacity posture; the parity-vs-superiority gap.
- Outputs: a crush / accommodate decision justified by precedent, a deterrence-posture plan, and a read on whether the entrant can clear the superiority bar.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- A cheap-to-beat entrant may still need destroying to deter imitators — judging on its single-handed threat alone misses the precedent.
- Maintaining excess capacity and full niche coverage is expensive and its benefit is hard to measure.
- An over-rigid "always fight" posture can be value-destroying once entry has occurred; misreading replicability inverts the right call.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A defended incumbent let a unique, hard-to-copy entrant in because accommodation set no precedent; in a different market it matched every move of a deep-pocketed challenger whose product was only at parity (not superior) and held its base — fighting the precedent the second entry would have set.

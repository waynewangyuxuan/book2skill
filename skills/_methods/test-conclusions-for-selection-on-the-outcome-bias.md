## When to use
Whenever a causal claim is supported by examples that were chosen *because* they had the outcome being explained: long-lived dominant firms, cautionary failures, or a survivorship-corrected estimate.

## Method
1. Identify what the sample is conditioned on (survival? success? memorability of failure?).
2. Ask for the missing cells: firms with the alleged cause that still failed; firms that succeeded without it; the failures excluded from a success roster.
3. If the proposed correction needs a base rate (e.g. a failure rate), demand the *actual* rate — a hypothetical rate ("if one in four...") yields only a directional conclusion, not a magnitude.
4. Reframe the claim as: would it predict the outcome *before* you knew which cases survived? If it's only ever applied after the fact, it predicts nothing.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: a claim + its supporting examples.
- Out: the selection mechanism, the missing comparison cells, and whether the conclusion is supported, merely directional, or unsupported.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Sometimes the full population genuinely isn't observable; then accept a directional claim but don't let it carry a precise decision.
- The point isn't that anecdotes are worthless — it's that a list of same-outcome cases cannot establish "always"/"never" or a rate.

## When to use
Evaluating a merger, acquisition, make-vs-buy, or any large premium-priced concentrated investment.

## Method
1. **Identify the only reliable synergy: cost savings.** Estimate realistic *annual* cost savings (joint scale economies, eliminated redundant fixed costs, transferable proprietary process tech). Treat revenue/brand/"strategic fit" synergies as ~zero unless backed by a transferable barrier you could not simply rent or replicate; competitive advantages do not travel across markets, and customer captivity does not travel in a deal.
2. **Capitalize them:** cumulative discounted savings ≈ annual savings ÷ discount rate.
3. **Reduce to one inequality:** is the premium (plus fees) LESS than the capitalized savings? If not, the deal destroys value — stop.
4. **Subtract the contracting baseline.** Ask whether arm's-length cooperation or a contract could capture most of the same gains without the premium; the premium is justified only by the increment beyond that.
5. **Account for management attention** as a scarce resource: gains at the target often come at the expense of the acquirer's own operations, and the deal process itself consumes attention. Count only the net improvement.
6. **Read the payment currency.** Stock instead of cash signals management believes its own shares are overvalued. A deal that fails on cash terms but is done in stock tells you the acquirer's stock is rich (and the acquirer is effectively shorting its own equity).

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: realistic annual cost savings (discounted); the premium plus fees; the contracting alternative; payment currency; the management-attention cost.
- Outputs: capitalized-savings vs premium, an approve/reject verdict with the binding gate identified, and a read on the acquirer's own valuation from the currency choice.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Revenue synergies and brand-image transfer are the usual mirages; only cost savings reliably clear the premium.
- Premiums tend to be paid at cyclical highs; the savings estimate must clear an inflated price.
- Stock-for-stock can rescue an economically bad deal for the acquirer with inflated currency, but it is a warning sign to the target.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A ~$50B premium justified by ~$600M/yr of savings capitalizes to under ~$10B — far below the premium, so it destroys value on cash terms. It was still profitable for the acquirer only because it paid with grossly inflated stock — which is itself the tell that its shares were overvalued.

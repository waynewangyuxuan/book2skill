## When to use
Evaluating an industry that looks like a value trap — commodity product, overcapacity, declining or doomed demand, bad press.

## Method
1. Separate decline from unprofitability: check whether something closes the arena to new entry (often the very regulation killing demand, or a credible do-not-enter signal).
2. If entry is closed and only a few incumbents remain, recognize the structure as potentially high-return — no new entrants, so incumbents can harvest the shrinking market.
3. Look for the enabling coordination mechanisms: self-binding pricing discipline (uniform delivered pricing, most-favored-nation clauses, advance-notice of changes) plus joint sourcing that routes output to the lowest-cost plant — which causes the highest-cost capacity to be shut first as demand falls.
4. Treat "declining + closed to entry + few disciplined incumbents" as a profit reference class, not a trap.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: a declining industry + its entry conditions + incumbent count and discipline.
- Out: a re-classification (harvestable vs trap) and the capacity-retirement mechanism to exploit.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- If entry is NOT actually closed, declining demand plus overcapacity is genuinely a trap.
- Requires enough incumbent discipline (or few enough players) to avoid a terminal price war.
- Coordination tactics can attract antitrust scrutiny even when individually legal.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
Makers of a chemical additive being phased out by regulation earned high margins for two decades: the regulation that doomed demand also barred new entrants, and joint sourcing routed production to the cheapest plant so the worst-situated capacity closed first as the market shrank.

## When to use
A rival undercuts you (or competes across multiple fronts) and you must decide *where* to respond so the pain lands disproportionately on them.

## Method
1. For each candidate market, compute cost_to_me = price_cut × my_volume and cost_to_rival = price_cut × rival_volume.
2. Choose the front that maximizes (rival cost − my cost) — where the rival's volume is large and yours is small. Cutting in your own stronghold is quantitatively self-harming: your large volume multiplies your loss. This inverts the naive instinct to defend your stronghold hard / attack the rival where it is weak.
3. Make the response immediate, ideally automatic, so the deviator never profits from cutting.
4. Always pair punishment with a credible off-ramp — a signal of willingness to return to higher prices (e.g. public price-match policies, leading a visible price increase) — so the industry doesn't get stuck low. Pure retaliation locks everyone in the low-price trap.
5. To threaten the rival's home turf without cannibalizing your flagship, deploy a separate low-margin "fighter" offering aimed there.
6. Read each rival's reward culture: firms rewarded on share/volume/beating-rivals will price-war relentlessly (cooperation hopeless); only profit-focused cultures can sustain high prices. The goal is to restore price discipline, not to win share.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: your and the rival's unit volumes per market; the contemplated price-cut size; customer-quality segmentation; available fighter options; the rival's reward culture.
- Outputs: the retaliation front, the asymmetry of pain it produces, an off-ramp signal, and a forecast of whether cooperation is attainable.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Retaliating in your own stronghold is the classic self-inflicted wound.
- Signaling a price increase privately (calls, meetings) is illegal collusion; use lawful public signals.
- Works within barriers to entry; against an endless supply of new entrants, price/capacity discipline can't hold.
- A relative-performance-driven rival may fight past economic rationality.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
An incumbent selling 2M units faces an interloper at 400K: a $1 cut in the incumbent's stronghold costs it $2M but the interloper only $400K — so it strikes instead in a market where the interloper is the large player and it is small, reversing the pain ratio, and uses a reformulated fighter brand to threaten the rival's turf without lowering its flagship price.

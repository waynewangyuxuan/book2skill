## When to use
Assessing whether an asset-producing position holds a structural advantage, separating a prestige-bearing asset's surface value from its economic value, and judging whether growth there will create value.

## Method
1. Compute two quantities: the reproduction cost of the asset base (what it would cost to recreate its economic function efficiently) and the value of the earnings those assets produce (earnings-power-value).
2. Apply the equilibrium identity: in a freely-entered arena the two converge — any excess earnings-value invites entry that competes it away, dragging earnings-value back to reproduction cost.
3. Therefore a durable excess of earnings-value over reproduction cost is itself proof of, and a measure of, a structural barrier; the gap localizes and sizes the protected advantage. Convergence confirms commodity economics; earnings-value below reproduction cost points to deficient management.
4. **Separate surface value from economic value.** How much premium/recognition an asset commands (surface) is distinct from whether it earns above the cost of capital (economic). They diverge sharply; judge by economic value, and trace WHICH mechanism (customer captivity, economies of scale) converts surface value into economic value. If none does, the prestige is not an advantage.
5. **Condition growth on the gap:** growth creates value only when earnings-value exceeds reproduction cost (a barrier exists); on a level playing field it is value-neutral; under a disadvantage it destroys value. Never treat growth as intrinsically good.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: an asset base + the value of its earnings + (for prestige assets) evidence of premium and owner returns + a growth question.
- Outputs: a barrier verdict with a sized advantage (the gap), separate surface/economic readings with the conversion mechanism, and a signed value-of-growth judgment.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Reproduction cost of intangibles is hard to estimate; a valuation dominated by intangibles is less trustworthy.
- Use the right asset value (reproduction vs liquidation) per the industry's viability.
- The identity is a long-run equilibrium; short-run dislocations can temporarily break it.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A famous, beloved asset commands a large premium yet earns nothing above the cost of capital — high surface value, no economic value, because no captivity or scale converts the recognition into excess returns; a low-profile asset behind a real barrier shows earnings-value persistently above reproduction cost, sizing its advantage by the gap.

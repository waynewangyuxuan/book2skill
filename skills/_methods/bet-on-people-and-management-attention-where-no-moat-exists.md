## When to use
Evaluating an early-stage venture in a new (no-moat-yet) market, a brand extension into a competitive market, or any position in an industry without structural barriers.

## Method
1. For greenfield ventures, discount the business plan as a differentiator — plans are non-defensible, and the most attractive opportunities draw the most lookalike entrants, lowering each one's odds. Weight instead the sponsor/team's industry knowledge, contact network, and ability to recruit and re-aim the plan toward defensible niches.
2. For brand extensions into competitive markets, value the move only at the cost savings from not building a brand from scratch; require survivorship-adjusted brand ROI (include the failed attempts) and remember recognition is not a moat — only captivity-plus-scale is.
3. In any no-moat market, make operational management quality the thesis: leader-vs-laggard cost/productivity gaps of 2–3× persist for years and are driven by management attention, not by technology, capital, or labor quality (all broadly available). Look for a simple, clear strategic focus and sustained efficiency discipline.
4. Treat any diversion of management attention — succession contests, firefighting, unfocused diversification, sprawl — as a sell signal, because productivity gains stall the moment focus moves elsewhere.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: the team's industry network/judgment; the survivorship-adjusted economics of a brand extension; evidence of focus vs sprawl; signs of attention diversion.
- Outputs: a people-and-focus-weighted bet, a cost-savings-only valuation of brand moves, and an attention-diversion-based exit trigger.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Treating a large obvious TAM as a positive for a greenfield bet is backwards — it draws crowds of identical entrants.
- Survivorship bias makes brand ROI look like a moat when it isn't; always load in the failed launches.
- Assumes management quality and focus are observable; in opaque situations the thesis is hard to verify.

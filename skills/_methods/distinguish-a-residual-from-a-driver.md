# Distinguish a residual from a driver

## When to use
When a praised or feared metric is treated as the engine of a system, but it may be the arithmetic leftover of other forced quantities — or when "largest contributor" is conflated with "net contributor."

## Method
1. Check whether the salient metric is defined as the gap/leftover of two other quantities; if so, it is a residual, and reading it as competence or agency inverts cause and effect.
2. Redirect analysis to the drivers that actually set it.
3. For contribution claims, identify the binding scarce resource and net what the entity supplies against what it absorbs of it.
4. Conclude on the net; a large gross share can be a net drain.

## Inputs -> Outputs
Inputs: the salient metric; its definition; the binding scarce resource. Outputs: residual-vs-driver verdict; net (signed) contribution.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Requires correctly identifying the binding scarce resource.
- Net accounting needs both supply and absorption measured.

## Worked example
A celebrated high rate is shown to be the residual of forced production-minus-consumption, so it marks fragility, not skill; analysis moves to its drivers.

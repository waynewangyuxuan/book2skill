## When to use
When something is labeled durable / sustainable / a moat and must support a decision with a time horizon — valuing a protected business, or judging how long an edge persists.

## Method
1. **Adopt decay-by-default.** Put the burden of proof on durability, not on decay. "Sustainable" with no stated half-life is a mood, not a parameter.
2. **Identify the source and attach the matching decay clock:**
   - Differentiation / brand: low base rate of sustained excess returns; absent an entry barrier, margins erode as entrants fragment volume while fixed differentiation costs stay constant.
   - Demand-side captivity (habit / switching / search): discount for cohort turnover — how fast captive customers exit (move, age, churn) and are replaced by uncommitted newcomers; with no intergenerational transfer, the moat decays.
   - Scale advantage: market size and growth are inversely related to moat strength — large/fast-growing markets shrink the leader's fixed-cost-per-unit edge and lower the entrant's break-in share. Be skeptical of "huge TAM" as a positive for an incumbent.
   - Technology / first-mover edge: net two opposing forces — learning-curve gain (pro-pioneer) vs vintage penalty (later entrants buy cheaper, newer capacity). Fast growth lets followers ride the learning curve quickly and amplifies the vintage gain, so the lead evaporates faster; slow/niche markets preserve it.
   - Complex / support-intensive product: track convergent commoditization signals — rising reliability/ease-of-use, falling support intensity, cross-vendor standardization, declining R&D intensity, growing customer confidence to switch. When several trend together, captivity and scale are dissolving.
3. **Force a half-life (or a range).** An advantage without a decay rate cannot be matched to a decision horizon.
4. **Check the sign, not just the magnitude.** Some "advantages" net to a disadvantage in a regime (e.g. first-mover in a fast-growing market); find the external parameter that controls the net sign.
5. **Match half-life to horizon.** A moat shorter than your payback is not a moat for you. Flag any expansion into arenas where the advantage does not travel as decay/value-destruction, not growth.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: the named edge + its source; cohort turnover; market size and growth; the maturation/commoditization signals; the decision horizon.
- Outputs: an estimated survival horizon (or "unstated → unusable"), the sign-controlling parameter, and a go/no-go on whether the advantage outlasts the horizon.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Don't apply the erosion base rate where a genuine entry barrier (captivity + local scale) actively resists maturation.
- Half-life estimation is itself uncertain; prefer a range and test the decision against both ends.
- Don't treat all decay as fatal — a 20-year half-life is real for a 5-year decision; the error is leaving the rate unspoken.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A learning-curve leader in a rapidly exploding market loses its edge within a couple of years as later entrants reach the same yield floor on newer, cheaper equipment — it would have done better owning a slow niche for years. The "first-mover advantage" was real but net-negative once the growth rate flipped its sign.

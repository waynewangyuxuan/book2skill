# Classify behavior as discretionary or identity-forced

## When to use
When a scenario rests on a node "choosing" to do or withhold something — a funder threatening to cut support, a party threatening to stop buying — and you suspect the action is mechanically forced.

## Method
1. Write the identity that governs the actor's quantity.
2. Check whether that quantity is a free choice or a residual pinned by other flows the actor does not control.
3. If it is identity-forced, declare scenarios premised on the actor withholding it void.
4. Distinguish a discretionary support from a mechanical byproduct of the actor's own separate goal; only the former is a real threat.

## Inputs -> Outputs
Inputs: the actor; the governing identity; the threatened action. Outputs: discretionary vs forced verdict; which threats are empty.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- The actor may change the upstream policy that makes the flow forced; check whether that prior choice is itself feasible.
- Requires correctly identifying the binding constraint.

## Worked example
A feared "they will stop funding us" threat is shown to be a mechanical byproduct of the counterparty's own currency policy, so the threat is largely empty.

## When to use
You are an incumbent wanting to discourage entry/challenge before any specific challenger commits.

## Method
1. Make the threat credible by sinking it into observable, irreversible commitments: excess high-fixed-cost capacity ready to flood any demand a price-cutter creates; a single-product "we live or die by this" focus that signals ferocity; a visible war chest; fast-trigger response systems; few uncovered niches.
2. Understand the deterrence ideal: the best threat is the one never used — but credibility requires costly sunk signals; a merely-stated, reversible threat will be tested.
3. Weigh the standing costs: maintaining the posture is expensive and can lock you into irrational fights, so preserve an element of rationality — once an entrant is actually in, accommodation may beat an all-out war.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: your position + the credibility of your retaliation signals.
- Out: a set of costly commitments that make entry look unprofitable, with the ongoing cost of the posture accounted for.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Over-commitment: the deterrent posture's standing cost can exceed the value of the entrants it deters (which you can't count).
- A truly determined or irrational entrant may proceed anyway; then fighting to the bitter end can be worse than accommodating.
- Reversible/cheap threats lack credibility and will be probed.

## When to use
A few rivals are locked (or heading) into a prisoner's-dilemma-style rivalry and you want a structural way to raise joint profitability without illegal collusion.

## Method
1. Identify the dimension(s) along which the contested space can be partitioned: geography, product/field specialization, customer segment, or scheduling/time.
2. Assign rivals to disjoint niches along the cheapest available axis, including unusual ones (e.g. offset timing).
3. Confirm the partition removes the marginal-customer prize that drives defection — when clienteles don't overlap, each rival has little to gain and much to lose from cutting price.
4. Frame this as a cooperation mechanism (it can even increase customer choice), not a marketing tactic, and prefer the axis that also cuts duplicate overhead or enhances each party's scale.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: a set of rivals + the structure of the contested space.
- Out: a niche-partition design that lowers the incentive to compete and raises joint profit.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Some arenas resist partition (rivals must all be physically present in the same hubs) — then this lever is weak.
- Mixed cases (an item spanning two niches) need a value-based, not volume-based, division rule.
- Explicit coordination to partition can cross antitrust lines; the move must be reachable through independent niche choice, not collusion.

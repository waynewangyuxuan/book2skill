## When to use
When a move is justified by "synergy," "integration," vertical combination, or "easy extension into an adjacent market" using existing assets.

## Method
1. Set a low prior on the synergy claim by default.
2. For each link being combined, run the **two-branch barrier test:**
   - If the link's market HAS barriers, the firm already earns excess returns at arm's length, so owning/integrating it adds nothing.
   - If it has NO barriers, an internal transfer just moves money at market price (and if you're worse at it, you lose).
   - Either branch yields zero or negative net synergy unless ownership itself creates a NEW barrier — demand the claim name that new barrier.
3. Treat "adjacent market" as a hypothesis, not a reason: test each asset (brand, sales force, technology, customers) for whether it actually transfers into the new market's specific structure. Markets that look adjacent often differ enough that the incumbent beats you and your assets don't carry over. Captivity-based and brand-based "synergies" generally do not travel.
4. If a barrier does exist, restrict credible synergies almost entirely to cost savings (proprietary process adoption, eliminated redundant fixed costs, joint scale economies), and require their discounted value to exceed any premium.
5. Conclude value only when at least one part has a genuine structural advantage the combination extends.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: a synergy/integration/extension proposal + the barrier status of each constituent market + the assets it relies on + any premium.
- Outputs: a verdict (real value vs zero/negative), with the specific new barrier or transferable asset named, or the claim rejected — plus a cost-savings-vs-premium check.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Believing brand prestige or vague "fit" transfers; assuming adjacency implies easy victory.
- Overvalued acquirer stock can make a bad cash deal worthwhile as a stock deal — adjust when payment is inflated currency.
- "Better management" synergies are real but rare and bounded by the scarcity of management attention (withdrawn from the acquirer's own operations).

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A "financial supermarket" combination promised cross-sell synergy, but each constituent market either already had a barrier (owning it added nothing) or had none (the transfer just moved money at market price) — no new barrier was named, so the synergy was zero and the premium destroyed value.

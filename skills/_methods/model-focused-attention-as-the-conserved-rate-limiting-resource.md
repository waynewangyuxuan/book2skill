## When to use
Forecasting whether an improvement program (or any focus-dependent effort) will continue, or diagnosing why one stalled.

## Method
1. Model focused attention as the binding constraint on improvement — its concentration, not its mere presence, drives gains.
2. Treat it as a finite, conserved resource that cannot be split without loss: deploying it to one area necessarily withdraws it from another (zero-sum).
3. To forecast continuation, track the attention budget, not the technology or capital: predict stagnation whenever a competing claim on attention arises (leadership succession, a larger crisis, firefighting, integrating an acquisition).
4. Note that gains can vanish with the same workers and technology in place — so look to where attention went, not to resource changes, when explaining a stall.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: an improvement effort + the competing demands on management attention.
- Out: a forecast of whether it will continue or stall, keyed to attention allocation.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- "Attention" is hard to measure directly; proxy it by what leadership is visibly prioritizing.
- Some improvements become self-sustaining (institutionalized) and survive a diversion — the model fits attention-dependent gains best.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A productivity drive hit its target while management focused on it, then stopped cold for over a year once a leadership-succession process captured the same managers' attention — no change in workforce or technology.

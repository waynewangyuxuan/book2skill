## When to use
You run a small player that has found a profitable, tolerated position in a market dominated by larger rivals, and internal pressure to grow is building.

## Method
1. Once the strategy is proven (you're profitable and incumbents tolerate you), treat its constraints — limited scale, few routes/SKUs, single hub, narrow target segment, non-poaching — as load-bearing rules, not training wheels.
2. Identify the threshold past which growth converts you from pest to threat (the point where you start taking meaningful share from a single incumbent, or look like the first of many entrants). Do not cross it.
3. Resist internal pressure (founder ambition, employees' appetite for more) to scale past that point. Growth that breaks the constraints which earned incumbent tolerance is the primary failure mode for successful small entrants.
4. When you do seek growth, prefer attractive niches that are small relative to fixed costs, have customer captivity, lack a vigilant dominant competitor, and are extendable at the edges — and expand only at those edges.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: the constraints that earned your current tolerance; the share/visibility threshold that flips you to a threat; the internal pressure to expand; available adjacent niches.
- Outputs: an explicit "hold the line" decision with named binding constraints, or a tightly-bounded edge expansion that preserves tolerance.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- The instinct to "scale the winner" is exactly the kill switch — the strategy's constraints are what keep the giants from crushing you.
- Rapid growth also dilutes the very economies and culture that made the small operation efficient.
- Assumes incumbents tolerate you because you're below the threat threshold; if they tolerate you for other reasons, the threshold may differ.

## When to use
You are in a few-player market and want to escape a chronic price/feature war through structure rather than ongoing tactical combat — or you are the buyer trying to break suppliers' coordination.

## Method
1. As a competitor, restructure the geometry so you and rivals occupy distinct, non-overlapping segments — by territory, specialization, or even time slot. Where customer bases don't overlap, each player has little to gain and much to lose from cutting price; this stabilizes margins without illegal collusion.
2. Use self-binding structures that raise the cost of your own deviation and signal commitment: capacity limits, most-favored-nation pricing, narrow purchasing windows, and joint-sourcing/settlement mechanisms that route orders to the lowest-cost facility (which also auto-rationalizes capacity — the highest-cost plants idle and exit first without a negotiated shutdown).
3. Gate every cooperation/discipline tactic on a prior barrier check: limiting capacity or holding prices up only works if outsiders can't enter to capture the margin. In a free-entry market, restraint just hands profit to new entrants — abandon the cooperation play and compete on efficiency instead.
4. If you are the buyer facing tacitly cooperating suppliers, invert all of the above: demand private/opaque pricing, negotiate with each supplier separately, concentrate your business with whichever defects, and coordinate with other large buyers to keep suppliers from re-cooperating.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: the segment map and overlap structure; the industry's barrier status; available structural devices; your seat (seller vs buyer).
- Outputs: a non-overlap positioning plan, a set of self-binding structures, and a go/no-go on cooperation based on barriers (or a coordination-breaking plan if you are the buyer).

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Without a barrier to entry, every cooperation device is worthless — new entrants flood the margin.
- Direct collusion (meetings, calls, secret price-fixing) is illegal; the value here is lawful structure, not conspiracy.
- Overlapping or copied non-overlap can collapse if segments are too closely related to resist price temptation.

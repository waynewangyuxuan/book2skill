## When to use
Evaluating an attractive opportunity (a new market, venture, hot field) where there is no entry barrier — the larger and more visible the prize, the more tempting it feels.

## Method
1. Confirm there is no entry barrier in the target opportunity, so any clever plan is itself replicable.
2. Apply the inverse relation: a large, obvious, fast-growing prize is precisely what attracts many comparably-equipped competitors, driving expected returns toward the cost of capital. The bigger the visible prize, the lower any single entrant's odds — read an irresistible-looking opportunity as a crowding warning.
3. Weight the probability of winning inversely to the prize's allure.
4. Weight the durable edge toward the operator's scarce, hard-to-copy resources — deep domain knowledge, contact network, ability to recruit and re-aim a team — not the idea. Use a poor plan as a negative filter, but never treat a brilliant plan as sufficient; refine the plan and back the people who can execute and adapt.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: an opportunity + its allure/visibility + its entry conditions + the operator/team behind it.
- Outputs: an allure-discounted expectation of returns and an invest/pass steer toward non-obvious / operator-gated plays.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- If a real barrier can be built or captured early, the inverse relation weakens and plan specifics matter more.
- Execution excellence can still differentiate winners in a crowded field — the discount is on the average, not every participant.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
Several huge, obviously-attractive venture markets drew swarms of well-funded entrants with near-identical plans, and most lost money — the allure itself was the warning, and the few survivors won on operator capability, not on the size of the prize.

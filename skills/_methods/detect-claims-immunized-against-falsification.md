## When to use
Whenever a framework states a sweeping, confident claim and you cannot easily imagine what evidence would prove it wrong — especially when the claim is doing heavy load-bearing work for a recommendation.

## Method
Scan for four immunization moves and reverse each:
1. **Definitional collapse** — "X and Y are just two ways of describing the same thing." Re-separate X and Y; ask what distinct evidence each requires. If none, the "identity" is rhetorical, not analytical.
2. **No-true-counterexample** — a concept declared *always* illusory, where every success is relabeled to preserve the rule. Demand an *ex-ante* criterion that would distinguish success from failure before the outcome is known.
3. **Unbounded long-run convergence** — "eventually everything converges to Z." Demand a time horizon; a prediction with no horizon makes no risk-bearing bet and can't guide a finite decision.
4. **Catch-all residual** — a vague factor invoked to explain every case the main theory misses. Ask whether that factor is held to the same evidentiary standard as the main thesis or just absorbs all anomalies.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: a claim + the surrounding justification.
- Out: a verdict — falsifiable (and what would falsify it) vs immunized (and which move did it); plus the re-separated sub-claims that *can* be tested.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Don't over-apply: some definitional identities are genuinely useful conventions. The test is whether the identity is *asserted as proof* of a contested empirical claim.
- Hard to falsify in practice ≠ unfalsifiable in principle; keep the distinction.
- Risk of motivated skepticism — require yourself to state what evidence *would* convince you.

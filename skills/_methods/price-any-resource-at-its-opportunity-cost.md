## When to use
When someone claims an edge from access to a "cheap" resource (capital, labor, materials, time, attention), when an asset's worth is read off its book denomination, or when a "catch up to the leader's level of X" target is proposed.

## Method
1. Ignore the historical/accounting cost (or book denomination) of the resource.
2. Enumerate what the resource can actually be exchanged for or produce in its own context (its real use-cases).
3. Reprice it at its opportunity cost: the best return it could earn deployed elsewhere, locally.
4. Compare the actual deployment's return against that opportunity cost.
5. If the resource is deployed into uses returning less than its opportunity cost, that is a loss, not a saving — however cheaply the resource was obtained.
6. Reject level-matching benchmarks ("catch up to the richer comparator's stock") unless the local marginal value clears the cost; local conditions differing from the comparator's break the analogy.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: a resource described as cheap (or an asset read at book), its current deployment, its local use-cases and opportunity cost, and any proposed comparator.
- Output: a context-correct value and a verdict on whether using it this way creates or destroys value, measured against the best alternative return.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Failure: treating a low acquisition cost as if it justified low-return deployment.
- Assumption: a meaningful alternative return exists and is estimable; subsidized resources should be priced at the unsubsidized alternative.
- Local conditions may converge over time, changing the verdict.

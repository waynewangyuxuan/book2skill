## When to use
You hold the larger share in a market where unit costs fall with scale and a smaller rival is attacking or a niche is exposed.

## Method
1. Respond to every competitive initiative with a symmetric matching move — price cut for price cut, product for product. Because your average cost is structurally lower, a symmetric exchange of pain hurts the smaller attacker proportionally more. Deliberately accept margin pain rather than under-respond.
2. Never concede an unexploited adjacent niche. Treat it as a security hole: a niche gives an attacker the minimum scale to become cost-competitive and then expand at your edges. Value the niche defensively (blocking a beachhead), not on standalone profit.
3. Go on offense by escalating industry fixed costs you can absorb and they can't — heavy advertising, centralized capex, faster product cycles. This forces sub-scale rivals to either match and crater their margins or skip it and lose the new-customer race. Shift the industry's cost structure from variable to fixed as a weapon.
4. Size investment by absolute-dollar capacity, not by matching a rival's percent of sales: as the leader you can outspend in absolute dollars even at an equal percentage, and you win ties via customer captivity, so spend only enough to tie.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: your share vs the attacker's; the fixed/variable cost split; exposed niches; the rival's spending capacity.
- Outputs: a matching-response doctrine, niche-defense priorities, and a fixed-cost-escalation plan scaled to your absolute-dollar edge.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Only works while you genuinely hold the scale/cost edge — verify it first. A poorly-safeguarded scale advantage can be caught by a persistent entrant.
- Matching means accepting near-term margin pain; under-responding to "protect margins" is the trap that lets attackers establish a profitable beachhead.
- Escalating fixed costs hurts you too — it is a weapon only when your larger base absorbs it more cheaply than rivals'.

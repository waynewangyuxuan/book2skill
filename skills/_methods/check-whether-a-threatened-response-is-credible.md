## When to use
When a strategy depends on a committed future action that is against your own interest to actually carry out — deterrence postures, "we will always retaliate," promised punishments.

## Method
1. Identify the future action the strategy threatens.
2. Apply the **time-inconsistency test:** at the moment the threat must be honored, will executing it still be the payoff-maximizing move? If accommodation is cheaper than retaliation once entry has occurred, the threat is not self-enforcing.
3. If the threat isn't self-enforcing, treat credibility — not the threat's size — as the binding constraint. Rivals will price in your incentive to back down.
4. Look for mechanisms that make the commitment *actually* irreversible (sunk costs, contracts, reputation across many games); without one, don't plan around the deterrent.

## Inputs → Outputs
- In: a strategy relying on a future threatened action.
- Out: whether the threat is credible (self-enforcing or hard-committed) or a bluff, and what would make it credible.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Reputation across repeated games can rescue an otherwise non-credible threat — account for repetition before declaring it a bluff.
- Irrational/mission-driven actors sometimes honor non-credible threats; estimate how likely that is rather than assuming pure rationality.

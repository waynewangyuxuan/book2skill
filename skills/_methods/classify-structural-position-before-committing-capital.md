## When to use
Before allocating capital to entering, staying in, or exiting a market — especially when the instinct is "compete harder" or "differentiate to win."

## Method
1. Classify your structural position relative to the market's barriers to entry: are you inside the barriers (an incumbent with a durable advantage) or outside them (a challenger who is structurally replaceable)?
2. If you are outside the barriers (the disadvantaged player):
   - Do NOT enter on the strength of better execution, branding, or differentiation alone — none overcome the absence of a barrier.
   - If already in, plan an orderly exit that returns the maximum salvageable capital rather than fighting a war you cannot win.
   - Your only legitimate entry thesis is "the protected incumbent will mismanage its advantage."
3. Test any bargaining/alliance/threat play with a precondition: in the market's efficient configuration, do you have a positive role? If you bring no structural advantage, you cannot negotiate your way into value you don't add — redirect resources.
4. If you must operate as the weak player, make your strategy the active prevention of cooperation among the strong incumbents; your survival depends on their non-cooperative or irrational behavior.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: the market's barrier-to-entry status; your position relative to those barriers; the salvage value of your committed capital.
- Outputs: an enter / hold-and-defend / exit decision, plus (if weak) a "disrupt-their-cooperation" or "redirect resources" directive.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- Assumes you can correctly diagnose barrier status; misclassifying yourself as protected when you are not leads to funding a doomed fight.
- Fails when management ego treats withdrawal as defeat; the correct move for a disadvantaged entrant is often no action.
- Brand strength or differentiation can masquerade as a "position," tempting over-commitment; they are not barriers.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
A firm holding a small share outside a dominant incumbent's barriers maximizes value by exiting and returning capital, not by out-executing the giant — its only upside is the giant's self-inflicted error.

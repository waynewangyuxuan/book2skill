## When to use
When a player is "big" and you must decide whether that size is a durable cost moat or irrelevant bulk — assessing incumbents, would-be entrants, and the effect of a winner-take-share investment race (R&D, advertising).

## Method
1. **Define the relevant market** — the boundary within which the fixed costs stay fixed (a region, a product line, a network). Share outside that boundary doesn't count.
2. **Measure fixed-cost intensity.** Estimate the fixed/variable split. High fixed-to-variable → scale can matter a lot; low → scale is nearly irrelevant regardless of size.
3. **Falsification test.** If the marginal cost of the last unit ≈ the first (variable cost dominates, low R&D-to-sales), economies of scale *cannot* be the moat — rule them out and stop.
4. **Compute the cost gap.** The advantage scales with fixed-cost-intensity × relative-share difference within the relevant market (leader's fixed-cost-per-unit vs rival's).
5. **Per-unit form.** Amortized cost/unit = total fixed cost ÷ units; the ratio of competitors' per-unit costs = the inverse of their volume ratio — that ratio is the structural handicap.
6. **Investment-race form.** If both spend a fixed % of sales on a share-deciding investment, the leader's absolute spend = share-ratio × the rival's; compute the multiplier to predict who wins the next generation.

## Inputs → Outputs
- Inputs: fixed-vs-variable cost estimate, total fixed/development cost, each player's unit volume or share within the relevant market.
- Outputs: a per-unit cost gap (or spend multiplier) and a verdict on whether scale is a meaningful, defensible advantage.

## Failure modes / assumptions
- The decisive variable is *relative* share within the relevant boundary, not national/global size — getting the boundary wrong inverts the answer.
- Advantage requires *some* customer captivity alongside scale; pure scale with footloose customers is catchable.
- Assumes the cost structure is observable enough to estimate the fixed/variable split.

## Worked example (illustrative, single)
Two firms each spend 10% of sales on next-generation R&D. The ~90%-share leader thereby outspends the small rival roughly 9:1 in absolute dollars — making it the heavy favorite for the next generation even with no inherent tech edge.
